# Philosophical Boundaries of CTI

---

## The Epistemological Stance

CTI is built on an explicit epistemological commitment:

> *"CTI measures operationally verifiable cognitive transformations, not metaphysical truth."*

This is not a limitation to be overcome. It is a **design principle**.

By anchoring itself to the observable and operational, CTI avoids the conceptual traps that have historically hindered progress in AI evaluation: unfalsifiable claims, anthropomorphic assumptions, and debates about consciousness that cannot be resolved empirically.

---

## On the Frame Shift from v2 to v3

v2 of the CTI manifesto used "Law/Axiom/Principle" language borrowed from physics. v3 explicitly retracts that framing for three reasons:

1. **A law makes a non-trivial prediction.** The two CTI formulas are definitions that name ratios. They do not predict anything that could be falsified independently of the definition itself.
2. **Borrowing physics rhetoric weakens credibility.** It invites attack on the dressing rather than on the substance.
3. **AI infrastructure needs protocols, not theories.** TCP/IP, HTTP, and OpenTelemetry shape ecosystems precisely because they are protocols.

The retraction does not weaken the underlying work. It correctly positions CTI as what it is: **a measurement specification with explicit epistemological boundaries.**

---

## What CTI Explicitly Excludes

CTI does **not** make claims about:

| Domain | Reason for Exclusion |
|--------|---------------------|
| **Consciousness** | Not operationally verifiable with current methods |
| **Phenomenology** | Subjective experience cannot be measured externally |
| **Metaphysical intelligence** | No agreed operational definition exists |
| **Absolute truth** | CTI operates under bounded information and rationality |
| **Sentience** | Outside the scope of behavioral measurement |

These exclusions are statements about what CTI is qualified to measure — **not** statements about whether these phenomena exist or matter.

---

## What CTI Commits To

CTI commits to evaluating only:

| Domain | CTI Scope |
|--------|----------|
| Observable operational behavior | ✅ |
| Measurable decision transformations | ✅ |
| Reasoning under constraints | ✅ |
| Cognitive throughput (rate) | ✅ |
| Cognitive efficiency (quality/cost) | ✅ |
| Contextual utility of decisions | ✅ |

---

## The Behavioral Stance

CTI adopts a strict behavioral stance: a system's cognition is evaluated entirely by its observable outputs — specifically, by the decisions it produces, their quality, their rate, and their causal effects.

This is similar in spirit to:

- **Operationalism** in philosophy of science — define concepts by how they are measured
- **Bounded rationality** (Simon, 1955) — optimal decisions are relative to constraints, not absolute
- **Behaviorism** in psychology — though without its reductionism

CTI is not committed to any of these traditions fully. It borrows their rigor while remaining open to revision through the RFC process.

---

## Why an Explicit Boundary Matters

Without explicit philosophical boundaries, AI measurement frameworks tend to suffer from:

1. **Scope creep** — claiming to measure things they cannot
2. **Unfalsifiability** — metrics that cannot be proven wrong
3. **Anthropomorphism** — projecting human cognition onto systems that may be fundamentally different
4. **Metric gaming** — optimizing for proxies that do not reflect the target construct

CTI's explicit boundary is a defense against all four.

---

## The Conceptual Shift

Traditional AI evaluation asks:

> "Is the output correct?"

CTI asks:

> "Does the decision advance a defined objective, efficiently, given available resources?"

This shifts the frame from:

```
input → output (correct/incorrect)
```

to:

```
state → cognitive transformation → contextual utility (measured operationally)
```

---

## Open Philosophical Questions

CTI does not claim to have resolved these questions. They are open:

1. Can a purely behavioral measure of cognition ever be complete?
2. Is contextual utility the right proxy for decision quality?
3. What is the relationship between $I_t$ (throughput) and intelligence as understood in cognitive science?
4. Does the rejection of consciousness claims introduce its own biases?
5. How should CTI handle systems that produce valid decisions through processes that are not human-legible?
